# Worked Example: Monetizing the 8K Newsletter

> 🧪 **This is a WORKED EXAMPLE — a fictional but realistic scenario built to teach the decision math.** "Dina" is not a real client. Every rate and benchmark in the calculations is real and cited; the point is watching the three monetization paths get computed honestly side by side.

## ⚡ What it does

Teaches audience monetization as arithmetic instead of ideology. Three paths — paid subscription, sponsorships, own product — get run against published benchmark rates for the same fictional newsletter, so you can see why the "obvious" answer (paid tier) is usually the weakest at small scale, and how the real answer is a sequence, not a choice.

## 🎯 The Scenario

**"Dina"** — fictional creator of an Arabic/English marketing newsletter for e-commerce operators in Egypt and GCC. After a year: **8,000 subscribers** (almost exactly beehiiv's year-one median of 8,314 — [State of Newsletters 2026](https://www.beehiiv.com/blog/beehiiv-the-state-of-newsletters-2026)), 42% open rate, weekly send, zero revenue.

Three advisors, three confident answers: *"launch a $10/month paid tier"* / *"sell sponsorships"* / *"build a product."* Instead of picking a guru, we run the math.

## 📋 The Walkthrough

### Path A — The paid tier (the "obvious" one)

The folklore says newsletters convert 5-10% to paid. The audited number — beehiiv's data across 65K newsletters — is a median of **0.62%**, with strong niches like finance reaching ~0.8-1.9% ([State of Paid Newsletters 2026](https://www.beehiiv.com/blog/the-state-of-paid-newsletters-2026)).

```
8,000 subs × 0.62%            = ~50 paid subscribers
50 × $10/month                = $500/month gross
Churn reality: paid newsletter churn runs 5-17%/month by niche
  → at ~8%/month, average paid lifetime ≈ 12 months, and Dina
    must ADD ~4 paid subs/month just to stand still.
Plus: a paid tier means writing a SECOND newsletter forever.
```

Even doubling the median (aggressive) = $1,000/month for double the writing. **Verdict at this scale: real money, worst effort-to-revenue ratio of the three. Not first.**

### Path B — Sponsorships

Newsletter sponsorship CPMs run $15-35 consumer, $50-100+ niche B2B, and marketing/SaaS B2B can ask $80-200 ([Paved marketplace](https://www.paved.com/blog/newsletter-sponsorship-rates/) — asking rates; deals close lower). Dina's audience is niche B2B (e-com operators) in a market advertisers struggle to reach — the good end of the curve, discounted for realism:

```
8,000 subs × 42% open ≈ 3,360 opens per send
At a conservative $50 CPM on opens: ~$170/slot
4 sends/month, 1 slot each        = ~$650-700/month
Effort: selling + a 3-line ad block. No second product to write.
```

Comparable money to the paid tier at a fraction of the effort — **and it's the right FIRST move** for a second reason: sponsor outreach forces Dina to articulate who her audience is, which is exactly the asset the third path needs. Caveat worth teaching: sponsor revenue scales only with list growth, and single-sponsor dependence is fragility dressed as revenue.

### Path C — The product (where audience economics actually work)

The audience's trust is worth more applied to Dina's own offer than rented to someone else's. A digital product for e-com operators — say a COD operations toolkit (templates + the exact kind of scripts in this repo) at **$49 one-time**, built once from her best content (the [Lead Magnet Designer](../prompts/content/lead-magnet-designer.md) logic, inverted: the newsletter has been the free tier all along).

Benchmark anchor: email is the highest-converting traffic source in e-commerce at ~4-5% ([traffic-source benchmarks](../benchmarks/cro-ecommerce-benchmarks.md)), and launch emails to a warm owned list outperform cold traffic by construction. Modeled conservatively at 1.5-2.5% of the list buying during a proper launch week (a [Lifetime Deal-style launch wave](../playbooks/lifetime-deal-launch-playbook.md): story → objections → proof → deadline):

```
8,000 × 1.5-2.5%  = 120-200 buyers × $49 = $5,900-9,800 per launch
Repeatable ~2-3×/year with new cohorts + list growth.
One launch ≈ a year of the paid tier.
```

⚠️ Honesty box: unlike Paths A and B, this range is *modeled from adjacent benchmarks*, not from a published newsletter-product dataset — no audited benchmark for "launch conversion to own product" exists. That's exactly the kind of assumption you label before betting on it.

### The Decision (and the sequencing lesson)

Not A or B or C — **B → C → A**, in that order:

1. **Sponsorships now** (month 1): fastest to revenue, zero product risk, forces audience clarity.
2. **Product launch** (month 3-4): the real economics. Sponsor income funds the build.
3. **Paid tier last, if ever** (month 9+): only once there's a proven segment that buys everything — because a paid tier is a subscription COMMITMENT to produce, and the 5-17% monthly churn data says it punishes anyone who launches it on hope.

The meta-lesson: monetization advice fails when it's a slogan ("just launch a paid tier!"). It works when it's arithmetic against your actual list size, open rate, and niche CPM — twenty minutes with the [content benchmarks file](../benchmarks/content-marketing-benchmarks.md) replaces three gurus.

## 🧠 The transferable lessons

- **Run all three models before picking one.** The spreadsheet takes an evening; a wrong monetization bet takes a year.
- **Medians, not testimonials:** plan on 0.62% free→paid and celebrate beating it — the 10% stories you've heard are top-decile outliers in high-willingness niches.
- **Small lists monetize by depth, not tolls.** Under ~20-30K subscribers, one $49 product to 2% of the list beats $10/month from 0.6% of it — audience businesses are conversion-depth businesses.
- **Label your modeled numbers.** Two of the three paths above used published rates; one used inference. Knowing which is which is the entire skill of honest forecasting.
- **Run this on your list:** your subs × (0.62% × $price) vs opens × niche CPM ÷ 1000 vs subs × 2% × product price. The order the answers come out in is your roadmap.
