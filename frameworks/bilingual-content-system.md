# The Bilingual Content System (EN + AR)

> A decision framework for running English + Arabic marketing as one operation: three asset tiers, clear rules for what gets rebuilt vs. kept vs. born Arabic-first, and the register standard that keeps the Arabic sounding human.

## ⚡ What it does

Bilingual content operations collapse from one of two mistakes: translating 100% of everything (the Arabic reads dead, the workload doubles, quality halves) or improvising per-piece (the Arabic audience gets leftovers). This framework replaces both with a tier system — decided once per asset TYPE, not renegotiated per piece — so the operation scales without a second team.

## 🎯 When I use it (real scenario)

This is how my own properties run — Arabic-first commerce and content for the Egyptian/MENA audience, English for global reach and product surfaces, and deliberate rules about which is the source language for what. The framework exists because I kept relearning the same lesson: the worst-performing Arabic content I've shipped was always translated English, and the best was born Arabic — so the decision "which language is this asset's mother tongue?" turned out to be the highest-leverage call in the whole content operation.

## 📋 The Framework

```
THE CORE QUESTION (per asset TYPE, decided once):
"Where does the money/attention for this asset type live, and in what language
does that audience THINK about this problem?"

TIER 1 — BORN ARABIC (native, never translated)
What: ads, offer pages, CTAs, social posts, WhatsApp/email sequences to MENA,
UGC briefs, community content.
Why: these run on emotional register — عامية or nothing. An Egyptian reads
translated ad copy the way you read a scam email: something is off, close tab.
Rule: written by/with someone who THINKS in the dialect. English source may
inspire the angle; the words are born Arabic.

TIER 2 — BORN ENGLISH, REBUILT ARABIC (localized, not translated)
What: flagship educational content, frameworks, lead magnets, video scripts.
Why: the thinking is reusable across markets; the delivery isn't.
Rule: rebuild = keep the structure and insight, rewrite examples, currency,
platforms, and idioms for the market. Budget ~60% of original effort.
If you can only afford translation-grade effort → don't ship it in Arabic at all;
a smaller excellent Arabic library beats a large dead one.

TIER 3 — ENGLISH ONLY (deliberately)
What: technical docs, product UI (unless the market demands RTL), investor/B2B
material, global SEO plays.
Why: the MENA professional audience for these consumes them in English anyway —
your Arabic effort is better spent on Tier 1.
Rule: this is a choice, not neglect — revisit yearly as the audience shifts.

THE REGISTER STANDARD (all Arabic output):
- عامية (Egyptian default; White-Arabic lean for pan-Arab/GCC reach)
- English business terms stay English: offer, checkout, funnel, ROAS, launch
- فصحى ONLY for legal, formal announcements, and religious/greeting contexts
- The test: read it aloud. If you wouldn't SAY it to a customer, rewrite it.

THE OPERATIONAL RULES:
1. Every asset gets a mother tongue at creation. The other language gets a
   tier decision (rebuild / skip), never an automatic translation.
2. One idea, two builds: plan content at the IDEA level (one insight), then
   let each language build its own expression (different hooks, examples).
3. Measure separately: AR and EN audiences are different funnels with
   different benchmarks. A shared dashboard hides which language is winning.
4. The Arabic library compounds harder: less competition, more loyalty,
   AI answers cite the few good Arabic sources heavily (GEO opportunity).
```

## 🧠 Pro tips

- Staff for Tier 1 first: one native عامية copywriter beats three translators — translation is the one skill this system barely needs.
- AI does Tier 2 rebuilds well if you force the register: use the [Arabic Copy Localizer skill](../skills/arabic-copy-localizer/SKILL.md) rules rather than asking for "translation".
- The Arabic GEO gap is the quiet prize: AI assistants answering Arabic marketing questions have very few quality sources to cite — publishing Tier 2 rebuilds with proper structure makes you the citation.
- Never A/B test across languages ("the EN version converted better") — different audiences, different funnels; the comparison is meaningless.
- Watch your own code-switching in client calls and voice notes — that's the exact register your Tier 1 content should match.
