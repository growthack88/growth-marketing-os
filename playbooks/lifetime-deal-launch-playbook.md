# Lifetime Deal Launch Playbook

> Launch a lifetime or founding-price offer without cannibalizing your subscription business: positioning, price anchoring, the email wave, real deadline mechanics, and what happens the day after.

## ⚡ What it does

Lifetime deals are the most misused weapon in digital products: launched from desperation, priced by guesswork, extended past their deadlines until nobody believes the next one. Done right, an LTD is a cash-flow and community event — it converts your warmest audience, funds development, and creates founding users with skin in the game. This playbook covers the decision, the math, the launch wave, and the exit.

## 🎯 When I use it (real scenario)

Built from running founding-price and lifetime launches for my own products — where the structure repeats: a founding cohort price for the earliest believers, an explicit ladder to the next price, and a launch carried by email waves plus daily social posts rather than ads. The hardest-won rule in here is the deadline one: the single time you quietly extend a "final" deadline, you've repriced every future launch to zero urgency.

## 📋 The Playbook

```
STAGE 0 — SHOULD YOU EVEN RUN AN LTD?
Run it when: you need founding users + cash flow more than MRR optics;
your marginal cost per user is near zero; you have a warm audience to launch to.
Skip it when: your costs scale per user (heavy compute/support);
investors judge you on MRR; or you'd be using it to disguise a retention problem.
An LTD sells belief in the roadmap — if churn is why revenue is down, fix churn first.

STAGE 1 — THE MATH (before any copy)
- LTD price ≥ what an average subscriber pays over ~18-30 months. Below that,
  you're renting cash from your best future customers at terrible rates.
- Cap the cohort (seats or deadline — pick ONE mechanic and mean it).
- Model the worst case: if every LTD buyer is a heavy user forever, does the
  economics still work? If not, cap harder or exclude the expensive features.

STAGE 2 — THE OFFER SHAPE
- Name the cohort, not the discount: "Founding members", not "80% off".
  Founding status is the product; the price is just its expression.
- Anchor against the subscription: show the monthly price × years, honestly.
- Include: everything current + roadmap category access. Exclude explicitly:
  the things you can't afford forever (white-label, API volume, done-for-you).
- Founding perks that cost little: name in credits, direct feedback channel,
  price-locked upgrades. Belonging beats features.

STAGE 3 — THE LAUNCH WAVE (7-10 days, email-led)
Email 1  (D0):   The story — why this exists, why founding members, what the deal is.
Email 2  (D2):   Objections answered in public (the real ones: "will you still exist
                 in 2 years?" deserves a real answer).
Email 3  (D4-5): Proof + use cases — what early users actually do with it.
Email 4  (D-1):  Tomorrow it ends. Recap the stack, restate the ladder.
Email 5  (D-day, morning): Last call. Short. One link.
[Optional Email 6 (D-day, evening): final hours — only if your audience culture takes it.]
Parallel: daily social posts (use the Arabic LinkedIn Content Engine formats:
contrarian → story → framework → proof → CTA across the launch week).

STAGE 4 — DEADLINE MECHANICS (the trust ledger)
- ONE deadline, stated in every email, enforced to the minute.
- The price ladder is public: "Founding [X] → next cohort [higher] → final [full/subscription only]".
  The ladder makes ending the deal a promotion for the next stage, not a cliff.
- NO silent extensions. If checkout breaks on deadline day, extend loudly ("payment
  processor failed for 3 hours, deadline moves 3 hours") — precision preserves trust.

STAGE 5 — THE DAY AFTER
- Raise the price ON SCHEDULE. First person to buy at the new price proves the ladder.
- Onboard the cohort as a cohort: welcome them by name, open the feedback channel —
  the founding community is half of what they bought.
- Report back to buyers within 30 days: what shipped, what their feedback changed.
  This is what makes cohort 2 easier to sell than cohort 1.
```

## 🧠 Pro tips

- Sell the ladder, not the deal: "this is the cheapest this will ever be, here's the schedule" outperforms "80% OFF!!" with any audience worth keeping.
- LTD buyers are your highest-conviction users, not your cheapest — treat the cohort as a council and the roadmap gets sharper.
- Run the offer through the [Offer Stack Builder](../prompts/growth/offer-stack-builder.md) first: the LTD is the price mechanic, but it still needs a stack.
- COD/MENA context: lifetime offers work unusually well where subscription fatigue and card friction are high — one payment, own it forever, matches how the market already prefers to buy.
- Keep a written FAQ of every question from launch 1 — it becomes email 2 of launch 2.
