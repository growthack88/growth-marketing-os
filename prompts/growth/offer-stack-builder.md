# Offer Stack Builder Prompt

> Construct a complete offer from a bare product: value stack, anchoring, bonuses, risk reversal, urgency that's actually true, and a name — the difference between advertising a price tag and advertising a deal.

## ⚡ What it does

Weak offers make everything downstream expensive: ads fight harder, pages convert worse, discounts get deeper. This prompt runs a product through offer construction systematically — what surrounds the core product (stack), what makes the price feel small (anchor), what closes the fence-sitters (bonus + guarantee), and what makes it act-now instead of someday (real urgency) — and outputs the full offer ready to drop into a landing page.

## 🎯 When I use it (real scenario)

This is the construction behind the bundle and tiered offers I run for digital products — where the same core product sells at multiple price points depending on what's stacked around it (templates, workshops, community access, done-with-you layers). The lesson that shaped the prompt: the stack isn't decoration, it's objection-handling — each bonus should neutralize a specific "yes, but…" — and a bonus that doesn't map to an objection is just margin leaking.

## 📋 The Prompt

```
You are an offer architect (direct-response school). Build a complete offer around my product. Do NOT just describe the product better — construct a DEAL.

MY PRODUCT:
- Core product + normal price: [X]
- What transformation it delivers: [X]
- Audience + their #1 hesitation before buying: [X]
- My costs/constraints (what I can afford to add): [digital assets, time, support capacity...]
- Market context: [e-com / digital product / SaaS · market/currency · COD or prepaid]

BUILD THE OFFER:

1. VALUE STACK (3-5 components)
   Core + additions. For EACH component: name it, one-line value description,
   assign a credible standalone value, and state WHICH specific objection it kills.
   Rule: every bonus maps to an objection. No objection → cut the bonus.

2. ANCHORING
   - Total stack value vs offer price (aim: price = 20-35% of stack value, and every
     standalone value must be defensible — no fantasy "$997 value" on a PDF)
   - The comparison frame: what alternative spend makes this price feel small?
     (competitor, doing it manually, cost of staying stuck)

3. RISK REVERSAL
   2 options fitted to my market: one standard (guarantee/returns), one creative
   (keep-the-bonuses, pay-when-you-see-results, COD as the risk reversal itself).

4. URGENCY — REAL ONLY
   Look at my constraints and find true scarcity: launch pricing window, batch size,
   bonus expiry, capacity limit. If nothing is genuinely scarce, say so and build a
   deadline-per-buyer mechanism (evergreen but honest). NEVER fake stock counters.

5. THE NAME
   5 offer names: 2 outcome-based, 2 identity-based, 1 playful.
   The offer name is what people say when they tell a friend — test it out loud.

6. THE PITCH BLOCK
   Assemble it: 3-4 lines of landing-page copy presenting stack → anchor → price →
   guarantee → deadline. Ready to paste above the buy button.

THEN: flag the weakest element of the offer you just built and what would strengthen it.
```

## 🧠 Pro tips

- Digital bonuses (templates, swipe files, recorded workshops) are the offer-builder's cheat code: high perceived value, near-zero marginal cost. Physical products: use bundles and shipping upgrades instead.
- In COD markets, "pay on delivery" IS your risk reversal — say it explicitly in the stack instead of adding a guarantee nobody asked for.
- Run the output through the [Landing Page CRO Teardown](../cro/landing-page-cro-teardown.md) once it's on a page — offer strength and offer presentation are separate failure points.
- Tiered offers: build the middle tier first with this prompt, then strip it for the low tier and stack it for the high tier. Most buyers take the tier you designed best, not the one you priced best.
- Revisit the stack quarterly: bonuses fatigue like creatives do. Same core product, refreshed stack = a "new" offer without touching the product.
